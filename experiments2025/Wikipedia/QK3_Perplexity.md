Merenre Nemtyemsaf was an Ancient Egyptian pharaoh, the fourth king of the Sixth Dynasty, ruling Egypt in the early 23rd century BC, approximately for six to eleven years towards the end of the Old Kingdom period. He was the son of Pepi I Meryre and queen Ankhesenpepi I and was succeeded by Pepi II Neferkare, who may have been his son or possibly his brother.

His reign was marked by significant administrative changes: there was a notable increase in provincial administrators, while the central administration in Memphis declined in size. This shift increased the political independence and economic power of provincial nobility, who became responsible for tax collection and resource management. This led to the first provincial burials for high officials like viziers and governors of Upper Egypt.

Merenre's rule also saw extensive trade and quarrying expeditions. Large caravans traveled to Nubia to acquire incense, ebony, animal skins, ivory, and exotic animals. He even commissioned a canal to facilitate navigation into Nubia past the first cataract. Trade with the Levantine coast brought in lapis lazuli, silver, bitumen, and tin, while quarrying for granite, travertine, and alabaster occurred in southern and eastern deserts.

Merenre constructed a pyramid complex in Saqqara known as Khanefermerenre ("the appearance of the perfection of Merenre"), inscribed with Pyramid Texts. His black basalt sarcophagus was found in the burial chamber, and a mummy believed to be his was discovered there, although identification remains uncertain. After his death, he was honored in a funerary cult until at least the end of the Old Kingdom, and later included in a selection of past kings honored during the New Kingdom.

Overall, Merenre Nemtyemsaf's reign was marked by administrative reform, expanded trade and resource acquisition, and significant mortuary constructions reflecting royal ideology and religion.[1][2][3]
