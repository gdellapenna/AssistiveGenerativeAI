## Overall Layout
The Wall Street Journal homepage at https://www.wsj.com/ features a responsive, modern design with a clean navigation bar at the top, a hero section for top stories, categorized news feeds scrolling downward, and right sidebar elements for multimedia and promotions. Content prioritizes breaking news, business, markets, politics, and world events, with subscriber paywalls indicated by icons on restricted articles. The structure emphasizes scannability through typography hierarchy and section dividers.[6][11]

## Header and Navigation
- Top header includes the WSJ logo (left), primary menu with categories like Politics, Economics, Markets, World, Life & Arts (horizontal tabs or dropdowns).
- Right side of header has user account links (login/subscribe), search icon, and possibly a hamburger menu for mobile.
- Persistent navigation remains visible on scroll for easy section jumping.[3][5]

## Main Content Area
- Hero banner showcases lead headlines with large images, teasers, and "Read More" links.
- Below are stacked sections: "Breaking News", "Markets", "U.S. & World", each with 4-6 article cards (headlines, summaries, thumbnails).
- Articles use bold typography for priority; key icons mark subscriber-only content.
- Infinite scroll or "Load More" loads additional stories dynamically.[5][6]

## Sidebar and Footer
- Right sidebar contains video widgets, "Most Popular", stock tickers, and subscribe CTAs (often red-highlighted).
- Footer (bottom of page) lists links to About, Contact, Privacy, sections, and social media.
- Multimedia like videos appears below ads in side modules.[3][5]

## Screen Reader Structure
Screen readers announce the page starting with the main landmark (header nav), then skip to headings (H1 for logo, H2/H3 for sections). Use heading navigation (e.g., H key in NVDA/JAWS) to jump: main content under "Latest News" or similar; links are labeled as "Article: [headline] [link]". Tab through interactive elements: nav tabs, search field, article links; regions like "aside" for sidebar. Paywall notices read as "subscriber content" on restricted links.[6][3]
